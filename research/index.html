Research Experiences


I am interested in social determinants of health disparities and aging, with an emphasis on chronic stressors, social networks, and health behaviors.  I work with Health Retirement Study to explore how caregiving, education, and social support shape the cognitive outcomes and biological aging of older adults. By applying longitudinal design, causal inference techniques, and biomarkers like DNA methylation clocks, I investigate how caregiving and family dynamics affect cognitive and biological aging across diverse groups. My work aims to uncover the biosocial mechanisms driving health disparities and inform strategies to promote equity and healthier aging throughout the life course.


(1) Grandparenting and Health Behaviors 

Patricia Drentea, Greg Pavela, Lu Tian, and Julie Locher. “Grandparenting and Physical Activity.” Journal of Aging and Health DOI: 10.1177/08982643241273197

In this study,  I examined the relationship between grandparenting care and physical activity among older adults through. We created a novel continuum to measure the intensity of grandparenting caregiving, taking into account both household living arrangements and caregiving hours. Through weighted logistic modeling and moderation analysis, we found that individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds and racial minorities were more likely to take on custodial grandparent roles and reported lower physical activity. 

(2) Publications under Review 

Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea, “Family Members' Education and Cognitive Functioning Among Older Adults” (R&R with Journal of Population Aging) (code)

In this paper, I examined how the education levels of older adults’ parents and their adult children influence cognitive outcomes in later life. I used generalized linear modeling, inverse-probability-of-attrition weighting, and mediation analysis to address sample attrition and explore potential behavioral pathways. The findings showed that higher education among adult children is linked to better cognitive function in older adults, independent of the education levels of the older adults themselves and their parents. Additionally, the positive effect of education on cognitive function may be partly explained by more moderate drinking behaviors. This work received the William Cockerham Paper Award for best graduate student paper in 2023. 

Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea, A Biological Embodiment of Caregiving: A Longitudinal Study of Transitions and Gender Differences in Epigenetic Aging (under review with Advances in Life Course Research) (code)

Selection bias is a concern in caregiving research, since healthier older adults may be more likely to take on caregiving roles. To address this, IIdesigned a study using propensity score matching to strengthen the causal inference.  Using propensity-score weighting with a machine learning model to better isolate the effects of starting or stopping caregiving on DNA methylation age, I created new caregiver variables that accounted for both transitions into and out of caregiving and the intensity of care provided. The results showed that men experienced faster biological aging when they began high-intensity caregiving, while women showed a significant slowing of aging when they continued in moderate-intensity caregiving roles.

Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea, The Role of Social Ties in the Cognitive Function of Informal Caregivers: Findings from a Population-Based Propensity-Matched Analysis (under review with Journal of Aging and Research) (code)

This paper applied a causal inference approach to address selection bias, utilizing propensity score matching in combination with mediation analysis to investigate how social networks influence the link between caregiving and cognitive function among older adults. The results indicated that older caregivers tend to have a greater number of close social ties, which were significantly associated with improved cognitive outcomes. In contrast, neither perceived social support nor the broader composition of one’s social network demonstrated the same effect.

(3) Publications in Progress

Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea, Fei Wu, “Association between Informal Caregiving and Biological Aging—A Machine Learning-based Approach to Biological Age Estimation”

With HRS psychosocial and clinic measurements data, I plan to derive a psychosocial biological age from validated scales. I plan to train an elastic-net model to predict mortality and functional decline, convert predicted risk to an age-equivalent, and compute psychosocial aging acceleration. I also plan to test whether caregiving exposure is associated with accelerated psychosocial aging.

Lu Tian, “A review of Family Caregiving and Biological Aging.”

I’m writing a scoping review to understand how caregiving might affect biological aging, and I’ve focused on three broad and meaningful measures rather than a single biomarker like telomere length. The first is DNA methylation clocks—tools like PhenoAge and GrimAge that estimate biological age by looking at DNA patterns, and they’ve been shown to predict health outcomes better than earlier biomarkers. The second, intrinsic capacity, captures the combined physical and mental abilities a person can draw on at any moment—including mobility, thinking skills, sensory functions, vitality, and mood. And the third measure, frailty, reflects how susceptible someone becomes as their reserves shrink—a practical indicator of vulnerability in older adults. I chose these three because they’re better linked with health outcomes and lend themselves more readily to clinical action in geriatric care than narrow biomarkers like telomere length.
