Research Experiences

I am interested in social determinants of health disparities and aging, with an emphasis on chronic stressors, social networks, and health behaviors. I work with the Health and Retirement Study to explore how caregiving, education, and social support shape the cognitive outcomes and biological aging of older adults. By applying longitudinal designs, causal inference techniques, and biomarkers like DNA methylation clocks, I investigate how caregiving and family dynamics affect cognitive and biological aging across diverse groups. My work aims to uncover the biosocial mechanisms driving health disparities and inform strategies to promote equity and healthier aging throughout the life course.

(1) Peer-reviewed Publications

  • Patricia Drentea, Greg Pavela, Lu Tian, and Julie Locher. “Grandparenting and Physical Activity” (2024). Journal of Aging and Health. DOI: 10.1177/08982643241273197. code ).

    This study examined the relationship between grandparenting care and physical activity among older adults. I created a novel continuum to measure the intensity of grandparenting caregiving, taking into account both household living arrangements and caregiving hours. Through weighted logistic modeling and moderation analysis, results showed that individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds and racial minorities were more likely to take on custodial grandparent roles and reported lower physical activity.

(2) Publications under Review

  • Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea. “Family Members’ Education and Cognitive Functioning Among Older Adults” (2025) (R&R with Journal of Population Aging) ( code ).

    I examined how the education levels of older adults’ parents and their adult children influence cognitive outcomes in later life. Using generalized linear modeling, inverse-probability-of-attrition weighting, and mediation analysis to address sample attrition and explore behavioral pathways, I found that higher education among adult children is linked to better cognitive function in older adults, independent of the education levels of the older adults themselves and their parents. The positive effect of education on cognition may be partly explained by more moderate drinking behaviors. This work received the William Cockerham Paper Award for best graduate student paper in 2023.

  • Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea. “A Biological Embodiment of Caregiving: A Longitudinal Study of Transitions and Gender Differences in Epigenetic Aging” (2025) (under review with Advances in Life Course Research) ( code ).

    Selection bias is a concern in caregiving research, since healthier older adults may be more likely to take on caregiving roles. To address this, I designed a study using propensity-score methods to strengthen causal inference. Using propensity-score weighting with a machine-learning model to isolate the effects of starting or stopping caregiving on DNA methylation age, I created caregiver variables that account for transitions into and out of caregiving and the intensity of care provided. Results showed that men experienced faster biological aging when they began high-intensity caregiving, while women showed a significant slowing of aging when they continued in moderate-intensity caregiving roles.

  • Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea. “The Role of Social Ties in the Cognitive Function of Informal Caregivers: Findings from a Population-Based Propensity-Matched Analysis” (2025) (under review with Journal of Aging and Research) ( code ).

    This paper applied a causal-inference approach to address selection bias, using propensity-score matching together with mediation analysis to investigate how social networks influence the link between caregiving and cognitive function among older adults. The results indicated that older caregivers tend to have a greater number of close social ties, which were associated with improved cognitive outcomes. In contrast, neither perceived social support nor the broader composition of one’s social network demonstrated the same effect.

(3) Publications in Progress

  • Lu Tian, Patricia Drentea, Fei Wu. “Association between Informal Caregiving and Biological Aging — A Machine-Learning-based Approach to Biological Age Estimation” (2025).

    Using HRS psychosocial and clinic measurement data, I plan to derive a psychosocial biological age from validated scales. I will train an elastic-net model to predict mortality or functional decline, convert predicted risk to an age equivalent, and compute psychosocial aging acceleration. I will then test whether caregiving exposure is associated with accelerated psychosocial aging.

  • Lu Tian. “A Review of Family Caregiving and Biological Aging” (2025).

    I am writing a scoping review to understand how caregiving may affect biological aging, focusing on three broad and clinically meaningful measures rather than a single biomarker like telomere length. First, DNA methylation clocks (e.g., PhenoAge, GrimAge) estimate biological age from DNA patterns and often predict health outcomes better than earlier biomarkers. Second, intrinsic capacity captures the combined physical and mental abilities a person can draw on at a given time—mobility, cognition, sensory function, vitality, and mood. Third, frailty reflects vulnerability as physiological reserves diminish and is a practical indicator of risk in older adults. These measures are chosen because they are consistently linked with health outcomes and lend themselves more readily to clinical action in geriatric care.
